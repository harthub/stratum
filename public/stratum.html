The Bull Case

Structural demand from AI, EVs, and grid buildout is converging on the same set of minerals simultaneously. Supply response is measured in decades. Companies with permitted copper, gallium, or rare earth assets are sitting on assets that are strategically scarce — not just economically valuable. Tech companies are beginning to bypass commodity markets with direct offtake deals, locking in supply and effectively putting a floor under certain mining equity valuations.

The Bear Case

Commodity cycles are notoriously volatile. A slowdown in AI capex spending, a breakthrough in material substitution (e.g. GaN replacing GaAs, sodium-ion batteries reducing lithium demand), or a geopolitical resolution enabling Chinese rare earth exports could rapidly deflate the supply premium. Junior miners in particular are execution risk stories — permitting failures, cost overruns, and financing dry-ups are common even in bull markets.

GEOPOLITICAL RISK

The AI hardware supply chain has a single point of failure: China controls the refining chokepoint for most critical minerals, regardless of where they're mined. Mining % and refining % are two completely different numbers — and refining is where the real leverage sits. A country can mine a mineral but still be entirely dependent on China to process it into a usable industrial form. The matrix below shows both figures side by side. The gap between them is where geopolitical risk lives.

RECYCLING & URBAN MINING

The IEA estimates that scaling recycling could reduce growth in new mining supply for critical minerals by 25–40% by 2050. Urban mining — recovering minerals from discarded electronics, industrial waste, and end-of-life hardware — is emerging as a parallel supply chain that partially offsets the 17-year mine development lag. For investors, this creates a distinct equity vertical: e-waste processors, hydrometallurgy companies, and closed-loop material recovery operators.

The economics are increasingly viable. A tonne of circuit boards contains ~200g of gold — roughly 50× the concentration of a typical gold ore body. Copper recovery from e-waste runs at 95%+ efficiency in modern smelters. Palladium recovery from automotive catalysts and MLCCs is already a mature industry in Japan and South Korea. The bottleneck is collection infrastructure and regulatory frameworks, not metallurgical feasibility.

The bear case counterargument to the pure supply shortage thesis: if recycling scales fast enough alongside demand growth, the supply gap narrows without new primary mining. The realistic scenario is that recycling offsets a meaningful portion of demand — but AI hardware's rapid refresh cycles mean e-waste volumes are growing too, creating a feedback loop that benefits urban mining operators regardless of primary supply conditions.

      THE THESIS

      The AI infrastructure boom is not primarily a software story. Beneath every training run, every inference call, and every data center expansion lies a physical supply chain anchored in minerals extracted from mines in Chile, South Africa, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and Australia. The market has priced the software layer aggressively. The mineral layer remains structurally underpriced relative to demand trajectory.

      "The primary constraint on data center expansion by 2026 is physical mineral supply chain bottlenecks — not financing, not energy."

      — Enki Research, Feb 2026

      Consider copper. Each megawatt of data center capacity embeds 60–75 tonnes of minerals, with copper dominating wiring, cooling, and power distribution. Microsoft's 80 MW Chicago facility alone required approximately 2,100 tonnes of copper. Scale that across the projected 100 GW of new capacity by 2030, and the demand signal is structural, not cyclical.

      The precious metals layer is subtler but equally real. Gold's role in connector plating, silver's presence in PCBs and thermal interfaces, and palladium's ubiquity in MLCCs — the capacitors inside every motherboard, GPU, and RAM module — create a demand baseline that technology scaling can only increase. Palladium in particular carries concentrated geopolitical risk: Russia and South Africa account for roughly 80% of global supply.

      The commodity forex transmission mechanism completes the cross-asset chain. When copper prices rise on AI demand, AUD/USD strengthens as Australia's mining revenue improves. USD/CLP reflects Chilean copper output directly. USD/ZAR captures South African PGM and gold dynamics. These are not theoretical correlations — they are structural linkages embedded in how commodity-exporting nations fund their current accounts.

      The urban mining overlay adds a critical counterbalance. Recycling will not eliminate the supply gap — but it creates a parallel investable theme in e-waste processors and closed-loop material recovery. The companies building that infrastructure today are positioning for a market that scales alongside AI hardware obsolescence cycles.

      Meeting the supply gap requires more than 8 Mtpa of new mine capacity — averaging 880 ktpa of new mines per year through 2035 — at a cost exceeding $210 billion (Wood Mackenzie). China has already moved: it commanded ~50% of global copper project financing between 2019 and 2025. Western firms, constrained by ESG pressures and investor risk aversion, have favored M&A over greenfield development. The mining equity opportunity is concentrated in companies with permitted assets in late-stage development — those that can bypass the 17-year average mine-to-production timeline. Junior miners with copper, gallium, or germanium exposure at advanced project stages are receiving valuation premiums that reflect the structural supply gap rather than near-term production. The IPO pipeline in this space is the most active since the rare earth cycle of 2010–2012.
